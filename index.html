Epictetus

Be not elated at any excellence not your own. If a horse should be elated, and say, “I am handsome,” it might be endurable. But when you are elated and say, “I have a handsome horse,” know that you are elated only on the merit of the horse. What then is your own?

Epictetus

His Life

Few details about Epictetus's life are known, but these are the most well known and historically accepted:

  • Born in the ancient Greek city of Hierapolis around 50-60 C.E.
  • Was a slave as a child.
  • While still a slave, he studied philosophy under the Stoic philosopher Musonius Rufus.
  • Walked with a limp caused by either childhood abuse or joint illness (sources disagree on the cause.)
  • Attained freedom from slavery but was forced to leave the Italian peninusula in 89 C.E. when philosophers were banned by Emperor Domitian.
  • Formed his own influential school in Nicopolis where he spent the rest of his life teaching and discoursing.
  • Died around 135 C.E.

His Influence

Epictetus's influence was substantial during his life and continues to be so today.

  • Origen Adamantius
    • One of the earliest and most influential Christian theologians

    • Believed Epictetus was more popular than Plato:

      It is easy, indeed, to observe that Plato is found only in the hands of those who profess to be literary men; while Epictetus is admired by persons of ordinary capacity, who have a desire to be benefited, and who perceive the improvement which may be derived from his writings.

      Origen Adamantius
  • Herodes Atticus
    • Powerful political figure and teacher of Marcus Aurelius

    • Claimed Epictetus was the greatest Stoic:

      Allow me, mightiest of philosophers, since we, whom you call laymen, cannot answer you, to read from a book of Epictetus, greatest of Stoics, what he thought and said about such big talk as that of yours.

      Herodes Atticus
  • Marcus Aurelius
    • Known as the philosopher king and the last of the Five Good Emperors

    • Was thankful for having been introduced to the writings of Epictetus:

      ... I must thank that ever I lighted upon Epictetus his Hypomnemata, or moral commentaries and common-factions...

      Marcus Aurelius
  • Albert Ellis
    • American psychologist who developed rational emotive behavior therapy (REBT)

    • Was happy to bring more attention to Epictetus:

      I am happy to say that in the 1950s I managed to bring Epictetus out of near-obscurity and make him famous all over again.

      Albert Ellis
  • James Stockdale
    • United States Navy vice admiral, Medal of Honor recipient, and author of Courage Under Fire: Testing Epictetus's Doctrines in a Laboratory of Human Behavior

    • Credits Epictetus for making him a better man:

      I think it was obvious to my close friends, and certainly to me, that I was a changed man and, I have to say, a better man for my introduction to philosophy and especially to Epictetus.

      James Stockdale
  • Tom Wolfe
    • American author and journalist who wrote the popular book A Man in Full

    • In an interview with Elizabeth Farnsworth, he talks about Epictetus and stoicism:

      Right. Stoicism – a true stoic has no dilemmas... they came to Epictetus, and they told him the choice that he had – either death or shave off your beard – and they said, we’ll give you 24 hours to decide. He says, I don’t need 24 hours. He says, I’m not shaving it off. And they said, well, we’ll behead you. He said, go ahead; you do what you have to do, and I’ll do what I have to do; my body is only a bowl of clay with a quart of blood in it; and someday I’ve got to give it back anyway.

      Tom Wolfe

His Quotes

Quotes from Epictetus can be found easily on the Internet. Here are a just a few of them:

  • What is the first business of one who practices philosophy? To get rid of self-conceit. For it is impossible for anyone to begin to learn that which he thinks he already knows.

    Epictetus

  • When you do anything from a clear judgment that it ought to be done, never shun the being seen to do it, even though the world should make a wrong supposition about it; for, if you don’t act right, shun the action itself; but, if you do, why are you afraid of those who censure you wrongly?"

    Epictetus

  • These reasonings are unconnected: “I am richer than you, therefore I am better”; “I am more eloquent than you, therefore I am better.” The connection is rather this: “I am richer than you, therefore my property is greater than yours;” “I am more eloquent than you, therefore my style is better than yours.” But you, after all, are neither property nor style.

    Epictetus

  • It is the act of an ill-instructed man to blame others for his own bad condition; it is the act of one who has begun to be instructed, to lay the blame on himself; and of one whose instruction is completed, neither to blame another, nor himself.

    Epictetus
